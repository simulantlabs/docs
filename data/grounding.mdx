Synthetic research should not be AI roleplay. It should be a rigorous inference layer built on observed human evidence.

That is the standard Simulant is built around. Large language models make it easy to ask more questions, earlier — but plausible is not the same as valid. A synthetic respondent can sound convincing while being too polished, too average, too certain, or too dependent on demographic stereotypes.

> Can we constrain these models with real respondent evidence, measure uncertainty, and validate the outputs against human ground truth?

That is the methodological question. The companion white paper answers it in full:

## Not an AI panel — an empirical research engine

The strongest version of synthetic research is not “ChatGPT pretending to be consumers.” It is a research layer that sits on top of real respondent-level data.

Where suitable microdata exists, each synthetic respondent model is **anchored to an actual respondent record**. If a dataset contains 2,070 respondents, we start with 2,070 respondent models. We do not invent 10,000 fictional people to make the work sound more impressive.

The language model has an important role, but it is not the empirical foundation. The empirical foundation is the human data. The model's job is to infer how a specific respondent is likely to answer a new question, conditional on what that respondent has already told researchers.

Where no respondent-level dataset covers the population, Simulant uses the **generated** mode described in [How cohorts are built](/data/how-cohorts-are-built) — composition fixed by official statistics, individuals instantiated inside those distributions. Every audience discloses which mode built it.

## The problem with generic AI personas

If a platform creates a fictional person from a few demographic fields, then asks an LLM to imagine what that person would think, the result may be useful for brainstorming. It should not be mistaken for research-grade evidence.

The risk is not that the output looks bad. The risk is that it looks too good — fluent explanations, neat motivations, coherent attitudes. Real people contradict themselves. They are unsure, inconsistent, and influenced by context. A methodology that smooths all of that into polished copy can mislead decision-makers.

A more rigorous approach has to answer:

- What real evidence grounded this answer?
- Was the response inferred from an individual human record or from a stereotype?
- How uncertain was the prediction?
- Does the synthetic population preserve the distribution of the real one?
- Has the method been validated against held-out human answers?
- Does the system know when the source data is not strong enough to support a question?

## How inference works (excerpt)

At a high level, respondent-backed inference follows this flow — summarised from the [methodology paper](https://simulant.tech/blog/synthetic-research-methodology/):

1. **Anchor** every respondent model to a real human record (demographics, answers, weights, missingness)
2. **Retrieve** question-relevant evidence — not every field stuffed into a prompt
3. **Estimate probabilities**, not just forced answers (`Yes: 0.68 · No: 0.32`)
4. **Repeat inference** to measure instability (simulation uncertainty)
5. **Apply source survey weights** after inference to preserve population structure
6. **Separate** quantitative inference from qualitative expression
7. **Persist an evidence trail** for every answer
8. **Validate** against held-out answers real people actually gave

### Estimate and expression are separate jobs

When a panel answers a closed question, the quantity of record is a structured response distribution — per-respondent option probabilities — not a parse of the persona's prose. The verbal answer is generated as a *separate* artifact conditioned on the recorded response.

Analysis runs on the elicited distribution. The prose exists for qualitative reading and is never re-aggregated into statistics. That separation matters: a model's storytelling register systematically distorts frequencies.

### Simulation uncertainty is not a survey margin of error

LLM outputs can be sensitive to prompt wording and configuration. Simulant measures its own instability — top-answer agreement, response entropy, run-to-run dispersion — rather than hiding it. We call this **simulation uncertainty**. We do not present it as a conventional survey margin of error, because it measures a different source of uncertainty.

## Representativeness is not validity

A panel can match every demographic and political target exactly and still answer wrongly. Composition and response behaviour are different properties:

| Property | Controlled by | Reported as |
|----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Composition** | Targeting, raking, scorecard | Representativeness / calibration status |
| **Response validity** | Held-out prediction, post-cutoff benchmarks | Domain validation status |
| **Stability** | Seed and prompt replicates | Simulation uncertainty / precision |

Every result surface keeps these as separate questions — never collapsed into one score.

## Synthetic plus human research

Synthetic research will not replace human respondents in every context. It will matter because teams have far more questions than they can afford to field.

Use synthetic inference to:

- prioritise what is worth testing before human fieldwork
- explore follow-up questions between survey waves
- get more value from respondent-level data after a study
- compare concepts and messages quickly during strategy development

Then use human research where it is strongest — when the decision turns on a level that needs a probability sample.

The future is not synthetic versus human research. It is **synthetic plus human research**, with each used where it is strongest.
