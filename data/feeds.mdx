A **news feed** is a workspace-scoped stream of current articles pulled from [GDELT](https://www.gdeltproject.org/), filtered by the keywords you care about. Feeds let you ground a simulation in what's actually happening right now — the same headlines your audience is seeing this week.

## Create a feed

Go to **Feeds → New feed** and give it:

- **Name** — how you'll recognise it in the picker, for example `Cost of living · AU`.
- **Keywords** — comma-separated terms that select matching articles from GDELT.

Simulant pulls fresh articles when you create the feed and then automatically **every 6 hours**. You can also click **Refresh** on any feed detail page to sync on demand.

## What's in a feed

Open a feed to see its recent articles. Each item shows:

- **Headline** — links out to the source.
- **Outlet and published time** — where and when it ran.
- **Summary** — a short synopsis generated from the article body, so you can scan the feed without opening every link.

Articles are enriched in the background — headlines appear first, and summaries fill in as scraping and summarisation complete. Only enriched articles are selectable as test context.

<Note>
  Feeds are private to your workspace. The underlying GDELT data is public, but the feed you build (its keywords, filters, and article selection) stays inside your workspace.
</Note>

## Use articles as test context

The strongest use of a feed is grounding a [test](/guides/tests) in the current environment. In the test builder, under **Context → Custom**, pick a feed and select up to **four articles**. Those articles are passed to every persona in the run, so their reactions reflect the news your audience has actually been reading.

Because articles are stored with the test, a run stays reproducible — you can revisit the exact context the panel saw, even weeks later when the news cycle has moved on.

<Tip>
  Keep feeds narrow. A feed with two or three sharp keywords produces a cleaner shortlist than one that tries to cover everything. Create separate feeds per topic — cost of living, election coverage, a specific competitor — rather than a single catch-all.
</Tip>
