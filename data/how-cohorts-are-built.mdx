A [cohort](/guides/cohorts) is not a prompt that roleplays your audience. It is a calibrated panel of synthetic respondents whose composition is fixed by official and academic statistics before any generative model is asked to speak.

This page summarizes how that works. For the full technical specification — schema, sources, constrained generation, calibration, and contamination-controlled validation — read the white paper:

## Two audience modes

Simulant builds audiences in two ways, and they carry different evidence:

| Mode | What it means | When to use it |
|------|----------------|----------------|
| **Respondent-backed** | Each persona is anchored to a real respondent record from survey microdata on the platform | Highest-evidence mode wherever suitable microdata exists |
| **Generated** | Respondents are created inside externally fixed distributions from census, election, survey, and polling sources | When no respondent-level dataset covers the population you need |

Every cohort discloses which mode built it. A generated panel and a respondent-backed one are never presented as equally grounded.

## Distributions first, individuals second

The organising principle: **distributions are deterministic, individuals are generative.**

1. You describe the population in plain language in the [cohort builder](/guides/cohorts).
2. Simulant maps that description onto prepared statistical sources and targets.
3. Composition — age, region, education, income, and other spine fields — is set by those targets before any language model runs.
4. Individual respondents are then instantiated *inside* those fixed distributions.

That split matters. Compositional bias (the wrong people) is driven toward zero by targeting and raking. Response bias (the right people answering wrongly) is measured against external ground truth — never assumed away.

## What you see in the product

When you [build a cohort](/guides/cohorts), the builder and Deep Research report make the method visible:

- **Population description** — the plain-language brief Simulant interprets into structured filters
- **Region and demographics** — the hard constraints on who can appear in the panel
- **Characteristics** — attitudes and dispositions grounded in source data, not invented labels
- **Size and precision** — how many respondents you draw and the implied ±pp
- **Report** — a sourced narrative about who the audience is and what moves them

## Representativeness is not validity

A panel can match every demographic target and still answer wrongly. Composition and response behaviour are different properties controlled by different machinery. Simulant keeps that distinction visible on every result surface: composition is controlled; response validity is measured.

For how this connects to simulation design and validation, see [How grounding works](/data/grounding) and the companion paper on [synthetic research methodology](https://simulant.tech/blog/synthetic-research-methodology/).
